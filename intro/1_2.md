# §1.2 Inside out


There is a second, hidden thread. It took me longer to see it.

The thread is inside-out.

In the book *The Brain from Inside Out* [buzsaki2019], György Buzsáki challenged the dominant framework in neuroscience, which he calls outside-in. Outside-in typically begins with the external world: a stimulus, a behavioral category, an experimenter-defined label or precomputed template. It then searches for the neural correlate. To study memory, define a memory task, run the animal through it, record the neurons, find the cells that respond. To study visual perception, present an image, find the neurons that fire. Inside-out flips the direction. Start from the brain activity. Ask what structure is already there, before any label is imposed. Let the system reveal its own variables: its own geometry, its own latent dimensions, and then ask what those variables mean.

My mentor Gyuri dedicated a whole book to building this framework. What follows is my own version of it: what the framework came to mean to me, through the specific work I did, and how it shaped my methodology before I realized it had.

### You get what you measure

The problem with a pre-conceived label is that we will only find what we are looking for. Define a metric, record the neurons that respond to it, and we will find neurons that respond to our measure. The label we bring to the data is also a ceiling on what the data can tell us. We have, in effect, decided in advance what counts as an answer. But the neurons that did not respond to the label, the cells that were busy doing something else entirely, something the experimenter had no category for, are neglected or dismissed as noise. Templates impose the same constraint in a different form: when we match new observations against a pattern built from prior experience, the template determines what we can find. In both cases, the external structure we bring is also the ceiling on what the data can reveal.

There are at least two kinds of projects. The first has a linear trajectory: a hypothesis, a predefined label built around it, an experiment designed to confirm it, and a result that lands where expected. This kind of science is useful. It is how fields consolidate. The second kind has a non-linear trajectory. It begins, perhaps, with a question, but it ends somewhere no one planned to go. The great discoveries throughout history, the ones that reshuffled the categories and made entire fields realize they had been asking the wrong question, tend to be the ones that took us by surprise.

Claude Shannon defined information as surprise. He chose the negative of the log of the probability of an event as the amount of information gained when an event of probability p occurs. Because log 1 = 0, a certain event contains no information. A result that lands where we predicted, according to our predefined labels, carries little information by this definition. A result that shouldn't have happened, a cell that fires at a rate no label predicted, a population pattern that refuses to match any template we brought to it, that is where the real information lives. The inside-out approach frees us from predefined labels and templates, and in doing so, giving us back the opportunity to be surprised by our data.

### You learn from what you measure

Inside-out, as I have described it so far, is a methodological posture toward understanding the brain: resisting the impulse to impose external labels, letting the system's internal activity speak. Buzsáki framed this for neuroscience, but as I held one foot in neuroscience and another in AI, I began to suspect the principle was more general, not confined to the brain, and not confined to analysis either.  The same logic, I came to believe, applies when we train artificial neuronetworks, 

Analysis and training feel like different problems, governed by different considerations. Why would the same structural problem apply to both?

But consider what happens when we finetune a large language model. We define a loss function and the gradient descent algorithm brings the model closer to the objective. With the current standard paradigm, the loss function is almost always defined with respect to something external: a corpus of text that humans generated, preferences that human raters expressed, rewards that a separate reward model assigned. The objective function, like the label and the template, is handed down from outside. In other words, standard LLM training objectives are outside-in. Whatever internal structure the model has built, whatever its own representations already encode, whatever they already imply about self-knowledge and uncertainty, none of this was ever part of the objective function.

This is not an abstract concern. LLMs trained with standard objectives learn to produce plausible-sounding tokens, even when their own internal representations encode uncertainty. The outside-in objective never incentivizes the model to reconcile its outputs with its internal state. As shown in Chapter XXX, the result is a system that hallucinates, confidently giving wrong answers rather than acknowledging ignorance.

Inside-out training flips this. Instead of descending toward an external signal, the learning objective is derived from the model's own representations. What the system already knows about itself, the structure already latent in its activations, becomes the supervision.


### When the label fails

To illustrate how inside-out shaped the way I approach science, let me use Yang et al. (Science, 2024) [yang2024selection] as an example.

During my early PhD. I was staring at trial-by-trial firing of hippocampal cells as mice ran laps around a maze, trying to make sense of the patterns. I got stuck. What I expected to see was the canonical place cell: a neuron that fires reliably whenever the animal passes through a particular location, trial after trial, as clean as a textbook illustration. What I actually saw was variability. A few cells looked like the textbook. Most did not. The majority remapped, either rate remapping where the same cell fired at a different rate on each trial, or global remapping where the cell's preferred location shifted entirely. The label I had brought to the data, "place," or location on the maze, could not capture what was in front of me.

If space was not the right label, I did not know what the right label was. So I gave up on starting from a label. I went inside-out.

I turned to UMAP, an unsupervised dimensionality reduction method, and let the data speak for itself. UMAP did three things for me. First, it required no predefined label; the algorithm finds structure on its own terms, not mine. Second, it is a population method: rather than forcing me to make sense of hundreds of cells one by one, it represents the relationships among all cells simultaneously. Third, it reduces the high-dimensional data from hundreds of dimensions (one per recorded neuron) to two or three, so that we can easily visualize it.

What I saw surprised me. Because the individual cells had shown so many different kinds of variability, I had not been able to make sense of the data by looking at them one at a time. But when I colored the UMAP manifold by trial number, something systematic became apparent. The hippocampal population manifold varied according to trial sequence, not randomly, not noisily, but in a way that tracked where the animal was in time.

Had I held onto the label, I would have called this variability noise. Once I let it go, the system told me what it was actually doing, something I had not thought to measure.

### When the template fails

A second example comes from Yang et al. (NeurIPS workshop, 2023) [yang2023nreps].

The standard method for detecting memory replay during sleep is Bayesian decoding, a template-matching approach. The procedure has two steps. First, you build a template from awake data: for each neuron, you estimate how strongly it fires at each location on the maze, producing a map of spatial tuning. This is the template. Then, during sleep, when a sharp wave ripple occurs, you ask: given the pattern of spiking we observe right now, which location does this most closely resemble in the template? The decoded location is taken as the replayed location. In practice, this decoding is done at each time bin within a ripple, producing a sequence of position posteriors. A sequence of such decoded locations across a ripple event reconstructs a trajectory, the memory, supposedly, being replayed.

This is outside-in. The template, built from awake experience, is like the label. Every sleep pattern gets matched to the closest awake pattern; the only question is which one. But this assumption can be wrong. It is possible that some sleep patterns do not map cleanly onto any awake pattern at all. A template-matching method will never discover this, because it is structurally incapable of returning the answer "none of the above." 

The inside-out approach is to start from sleep itself. In Yang et al. (2023) [yang2023nreps], we applied UMAP directly to NREM and REM sleep data, without building any template from awake activity. What emerged from the NREM manifold was bueautiful: a maze-like structure appeared on its own, without any awake data to anchor it. The geometry of sleep spontaneously recapitulated the geometry of experience. In contrast, no such structure emerged from REM sleep. The REM manifold was something else entirely. Approached without a template to force a match, we could finally see it for what it was: interestingly different, and genuinely unknown.

### When the objective fails

The third example is CASAL (Yang et al., 2026) [casal2026]. Unlike the two examples above, it lives not in analysis but in training, and not in the biological brain but in an artificial neural network.

CASAL's inside-out move was to replace the standard loss function with one derived entirely from the model's own internal representations. Rather than backpropagating from the output toward what an external corpus or rater said was correct, CASAL computed its loss directly on internal activations, asking whether those activations reflected a clear boundary between what the model knew and what it did not. The supervision signal came from within.

Hallucination reduced by 30-40%, at 30 times the compute efficiency of LoRA-based fine-tuning. The gain did not come from more data or better feedback. It came from training inside-out. The model's internal knowledge became the teaching signal that shaped its outputs.
