# Finite and infinite Games

There is a third hidden thread. I call it open-endedness.

In 1986, NYU professor James Carse wrote a little book called Finite and Infinite Games. It opens with: "There are at least two kinds of games. One could be called finite; the other infinite. A finite game is played for the purpose of winning, an infinite game for the purpose of continuing the play."

I think of my PhD as an infinite game. Scientific discovery, in general, is an infinite game.

Thirty years after Carse, two AI researchers — Kenneth Stanley and Joel Lehman — arrived at their own version of the same idea. In their book Why Greatness Cannot Be Planned, they argue that human innovation progresses through open-ended search. Like infinite games, open-ended search is diverging, not converging to a fixed objective. Their book is about Artificial Intelligence, a field that runs on optimization: define an objective function, run gradient descent, converge to the minimum. Yet their central claim is provocatively anti-objective. Anti-optimization.

Before I read that book, I was a committed optimization believer. Reading it, combined with my time in the Buzsáki lab, I came to the realization that when everything we do is measured against objectives, we rob ourselves of the chance for playful discovery.

It points to something profoundly interesting: serendipity plays an outsized role in life and science. Aren't the greatest epiphanies — in life and in science — so often unexpected and unplanned?

More importatnly. It teaches me I don't have to passively wait for serendipity to happen. There's actually lot we can do to attract serendipity:

More importantly, the book taught me that I don't have to passively wait for serendipity to happen. There's actually a lot we can do to attract it:

1. Accept ambiguity. 

2. Be willing to pivot. The book draws on many examples of the greatest innovations in human history. The common thread is that the successful often wander from their original paths, whether those paths were chosen by themselves or others. Rather than absolute devotion to their original objective, their secret ingredient seems to be a willingness to make a complete 180 when the feeling is right. True innovation requires being comfortable with not knowing the final outcome. You should trust your gut when it tells you something important is around the corner — even if you can't explain what that something is.

3. Serendipity isn't only for the lucky few. Most importantly, the book convinced me that these kinds of stories don't only apply to the luckiest of the lucky. Serendipity isn't actually that picky.

I, too, have had, some small serendipities
