# §1.3 Open-endedness

There is a third hidden thread. I call it open-endedness.

In 1986, NYU professor James Carse wrote a little book called *Finite and Infinite Games*. It opens with:

"There are at least two kinds of games. One could be called finite; the other infinite. A finite game is played for the purpose of winning, an infinite game for the purpose of continuing the play."

I think of my PhD as an infinite game. Scientific discovery, in general, is an infinite game.

Thirty years after Carse, two AI researchers, Kenneth Stanley and Joel Lehman, arrived at their own version of the same idea. In their book *Why Greatness Cannot Be Planned*, they introduced the concept of open-endedness. Like infinite games, open-ended algorithms are diverging, not converging to a fixed ending or objective.

What is interesting is that their book is about AI, a field that runs on optimization: define an objective function, run gradient descent, converge to the minimum. Yet the central claim is provocatively anti-objective and anti-optimization. Why? It is because of the paradox of objectives.

Consider two thought experiments.

Imagine you are a research funder at the dawn of cooking, when food is prepared over open fire. You announce a grand challenge — faster, smokeless cooking — and over the centuries you fund only the most ambitious work toward it. You would have funded better hearths. You would have funded better fuels. You would have funded better stoves and ovens... But you would never have funded the microwave. Because the microwave didn't come from cooking. It came from radar. In 1945, a Raytheon engineer named Percy Spencer was standing in front of an active magnetron when the chocolate bar in his pocket melted. He didn't shrug. He sent for popcorn — it popped. He sent for an egg — it exploded. The path to the microwave ran through electromagnetic research, through a chocolate bar — none of which you would have considered relevant.

Second: imagine you are a research funder in the era of the abacus, and you announce a different grand challenge — the most compute per hour, per dollar. You would have funded better abaci. You would have funded faster mechanical calculators. You would have funded better slide rules. You would never have funded vacuum tubes. Yet the very first computers were built with vacuum tubes.

Both thought experiments illustrate the paradox of objectives: the greatest achievements become less likely when they are made objectives. Objectives can even become obstacles to more exciting achievements — those involving discovery, creativity, and invention. Why? Because the stepping stones to ambitious objectives tend to be strange. Radar is not on the path to better cooking; vacuum tubes are not on the path to better arithmetic. The stepping stones are never what you would predict if you were thinking only of the objective.

Beyond the paradox of objectives, the book revealed the outsized role of serendipity in life and science. Aren't the greatest epiphanies so often unexpected, unplanned? The common thread across history's most transformative innovations is often that their creators wandered from the path. The secret ingredient seems not to be devotion to a fixed objective, but a willingness to pivot — to trust your gut when it senses something important around the corner, even before you can explain what that something is. True innovation requires being comfortable not knowing where the path leads.

Serendipity isn't reserved for the luckiest of the lucky — it isn't actually that picky. My own PhD, it turns out, contained a few.

### Christmas Project 2022

It was the fourth year of my PhD, end of 2022. I had no first-author papers. I had tried many things: recording REM sleep in baby rats, training mice on complicated tasks to study generalization. None of it had led anywhere impressive. I was actually very close to giving up.

Then Christmas came, and with it my Christmas project. The rule was simple: forget any particular objective. Let curiosity guide you. No deliverable, no hypothesis, no pressure. Just play.

I started playing with the hippocampal manifold. I asked a question I had never thought to ask: what if I color the manifold by trial number? "It wasn't a logical thing to try, given what we know about the hippocampus, the natural choice would have been to color the manifold by spacial location.

I sat back. Then leaned in. I didn't believe it at first. The population pattern was extraordinarily structured. Individual cells had looked like a mess, remapping and varying in ways I couldn't make sense of. But at the population level, the manifold organized itself cleanly according to trial sequence.

In January, I showed it to Gyuri. Neither of us fully grasped yet where the picture would lead. Gyuri has a saying: "If something is surprising, it is usually wrong." So I spent weeks validating what I saw, testing whether it was an artifact of the particular dimensionality reduction method I used, trying many other alternatives I could think of. It held.

But validation wasn't the end of the story. The trial-sequence structure was only a stepping stone to something more interesting. The real finding emerged only when I connected the population pattern during waking to the replay activity during sleep. That connection eventually became the paper in Chapter xx. 

Two things had to go right. I had to let myself play in the first place. And once the manifold appeared, I had to trust that something was there before I could explain what.

### Christmas Project 2023

It was the fifth year, Christmas of 2023. We had submitted the memory selection paper and were waiting for reviewers. I had nothing to do.

So I started another Christmas project. ChatGPT had just become a cultural phenomenon. I spent the break learning about transformers. I stumbled onto mechanistic interpretability, an emerging field that asks what computations are actually happening inside a neural network, working from its internal representations rather than its outputs. My initial idea was vague: could transformers serve as a tool to study the interaction between hippocampus and cortex? I didn't know how that would work. I tried to explain it at my committee meeting. It was quite a disaster because I lacked a well-formed, concrete plan.

Still, Gyuri said: "Go ahead and do it."

I hypothesized Gyuri might be a secret believer in open-endedness. There is an abstract similarity between the inside-out approach and the spirit of open-ended search. Why else would he allow me, at a late stage of my PhD, to wander into a field that was new to both of us?

The journey led me to steering vectors, which offered something I had been looking for all along: a way to causally manipulate internal representations rather than just observe them. That became my first AI paper, and Chapter XXX of this dissertation.

Like the manifold a year before, it was a stepping stone — though I wouldn't see to what until the next Christmas.

### Christmas Gift 2024


The Christmas of 2024 brought two unexpected gifts.

The first was an internship offer from Meta. For a long time, I couldn't quite believe it. Even longer, I couldn't explain it.

A month before I started in March 2025, I was invited to give a talk at the Bau Lab at MIT, a group working on mechanistic interpretability. During the visit, I learned that my intern manager, Diego Garcia-Olano, had interviewed nearly everyone in the lab. Every other candidate had been a CS student with more LLM publications than I had. I was almost embarrassed to admit that Diego had offered the position to me — someone with far less experience in AI.

The reason he hired me, when I learned it, came down to a small piece of serendipity. He had come across my steering paper — the one that began as a Christmas project in 2023, in a field I had wandered into without a plan. It turned out to contain something he was looking for.

The second gift arrived at NeurIPS 2024, where I first encountered LLM-based open-ended research. I followed the trail into the emerging field of open-ended self-improvement in LLM agents. What it grew into is the HyperAgents paper, Chapter XXX of this dissertation.

I had not planned any of this from the beginning. That, I think, is precisely the point.



### Open-ended Algorithms

My own trajectory had been open-ended, which mirrored the very field I had serendipitously wandered into: the design of open-ended algorithms.


"Could we just code up a little version of an evolutionary algorithm inside of a computer, maybe put it in a somewhat rich world, hit play, sit back, grab some popcorn, and watch the fireworks?"
— Jeff Clune


Can we create an open-ended algorithm worth running for billions of years, one that innovates forever? This is, more or less, what people have been trying for decades. Something interesting almost always happens, but only for a little while, and then the system converges. The fireworks fizzle: diversity collapses, novel outputs stop arriving, and the algorithm settles into producing variations on a small set of similar ideas. We still do not have an open-ended algorithm worth running for even a year. None of them is open-ended in the way that Darwinian evolution is, producing all the marvelous diversity we see on Earth.

But the artifacts being evolved have, until recently, been simple. Something has changed.


"A continually self-improving AI is one that, once created, can autonomously and continually improve itself better than its human creators can improve it."
— Zitong Yang

What if we combine open-ended evolutionary algorithms with self-improving LLM agents? Open-ended evolution becomes interestingly powerful when the artifact being evolved is the learning system itself.

HyperAgents, presented in Chapter XX, is an attempt to construct such a system — open-ended self-improvement realized as an LLM agent.
