# §1.2 The hidden thread — Draft

<!-- Last updated: 2026-05-04 -->

---

There is a second, hidden thread. It took me longer to see it.

The name is inside-out.

In *The Brain from Inside Out* [buzsaki2019], György Buzsáki challenged the dominant framework in neuroscience, which he calls outside-in. Outside-in begins with the external world: a stimulus, a behavioral category, an experimenter-defined label. It then searches for the neural correlate. The logic seems natural --- almost unavoidable. To study memory, define a memory task, run the animal through it, record the neurons, find the cells that respond. To study perception, present an image, find the neurons that fire. The experimenter sits outside the brain with privileged access to both the stimulus and the neural response, and builds a correlation between them. Inside-out flips the direction. Start from the brain's own self-organized activity. Ask what structure is already there, before any label is imposed. Let the system reveal its own variables --- its own geometry, its own latent dimensions --- and then ask what those variables mean. This is not a rejection of external stimuli or behavior. It is a rejection of the assumption that the experimenter's categories are the right ones. After all, the brain did not read William James before it organized itself.

---

### You get what you measure

The problem with a pre-conceived label is that we will only find what we are looking for. Define a metric, record the neurons that respond to it, and we will find neurons that respond to our measure. The label we bring to the data is also a ceiling on what the data can tell us. We have, in effect, decided in advance what counts as an answer. But the neurons that did not respond to the label --- the cells that were busy doing something else entirely, something the experimenter had no category for --- are simply neglected. In other words, we miss the precious opportunity to be surprised by our data.

There are two kinds of scientific findings. The first has a linear trajectory: a hypothesis, a predefined label built around it, an experiment designed to confirm it, and a result that lands where expected. This kind of science is useful. It is how fields consolidate. But it is almost always incremental. The second kind has a non-linear trajectory. It begins, perhaps, with a question, but it ends somewhere no one planned to go. The great discoveries throughout history --- the ones that reshuffled the categories, that made entire fields realize they had been asking the wrong question --- tend to be the ones that took us by surprise.

Claude Shannon defined information as surprise. He chose the negative of the log of the probability of an event as the amount of information gained when an event of probability $p$ occurs. Because $\log 1 = 0$, a certain event contains no information. A result that lands where we predicted, according to our predefined labels, carries little information by this definition. A result that shouldn't have happened --- a cell that fires at a rate no label predicted, a population pattern that refuses to match any template we brought to it --- that is where the real information lives. The inside-out approach frees us from predefined labels, and in doing so, increases our chance of being surprised.

### When the label fails

To illustrate how inside-out shaped the way I approach science, let me use Yang et al. (Science, 2024) [yang2024selection] as an example.

I was staring at trial-by-trial firing of hippocampal cells as mice ran laps around a maze, trying to make sense of the patterns. I got stuck. What I expected to see was the canonical place cell: a neuron that fires reliably whenever the animal passes through a particular location, trial after trial, as clean as a textbook illustration. What I actually saw was variability. A few cells looked like the textbook. Most did not. The majority remapped --- either rate remapping, where the same cell fired at a different rate on each trial, or global remapping, where the cell's preferred location shifted entirely. The label I had brought to the data --- "place," or location on the maze --- could not capture what was in front of me.

If space was not the right label, I did not know what the right label was. So I gave up on starting from a label. I went inside-out.

I turned to UMAP, an unsupervised dimensionality reduction method, and let the data speak for itself. UMAP did three things for me. First, it required no predefined label --- the algorithm finds structure on its own terms, not mine. Second, it is a population method: rather than forcing me to make sense of hundreds of cells one by one, it represents the relationships among all cells simultaneously. Third, it reduces the data from hundreds of dimensions --- one per recorded neuron --- to two or three, so that a human can easily visualize it.

What I saw surprised me. Because the individual cells had shown so many different kinds of variability, I had not been able to make sense of the data by looking at them one at a time. But when I colored the UMAP manifold by trial number, something systematic emerged. The hippocampal population manifold varied according to trial sequence --- not randomly, not noisily, but in a way that tracked where the animal was in time.

### When the template fails

A second example comes from Yang et al. (NeurIPS workshop, 2023) [yang2023nreps].

The standard method for detecting memory replay during sleep is Bayesian decoding --- a template-matching approach. The procedure has two steps. First, you build a template from awake data: for each neuron, you estimate how strongly it fires at each location on the maze, producing a map of spatial tuning. This is the prior. Then, during sleep, when a sharp wave ripple occurs, you ask: given the pattern of spiking we observe right now, which location does this most closely resemble in the template (expectation maximization)? The decoded location is taken as the replayed location. In practice, this decoding is done at each time bin within a ripple, producing a sequence of position posteriors. A sequence of such decoded locations across a ripple event reconstructs a trajectory --- the memory, supposedly, being replayed.

This is outside-in. The template, built from awake experience, is the label. Every sleep pattern gets matched to the closest awake pattern; the only question is which one. But this assumption can be wrong. It is possible that some sleep patterns do not map cleanly onto any awake pattern at all. A template-matching method will never discover this, because it is structurally incapable of returning the answer "none of the above." The label is the ceiling again.

The inside-out approach is to start from sleep itself. In Yang et al. (2023) [yang2023nreps], we applied UMAP directly to NREM and REM sleep data, without building any template from awake activity. What emerged from the NREM manifold surprised us: a maze-like structure appeared on its own, without any awake data to anchor it. The geometry of sleep spontaneously recapitulated the geometry of experience. In contrast, no such structure emerged from REM sleep. The REM manifold was something else entirely --- which, approached without a template to force a match, we could finally see for what it was: genuinely different, and genuinely unknown.
