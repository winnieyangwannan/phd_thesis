# Sam Zheng Writing Style Guide
*Extracted from Chapter 1 (Introduction) and Chapter 7 (Concluding Discussions) of "Neural Data Analysis from Inside Out," NYU Thesis, 2026*

---

## What this is

A style prompt for writing thesis chapters — particularly the introduction and discussion — that captures Sam's voice: first-person, intellectually adventurous, warm, and genuinely curious. His writing makes you want to keep reading and makes you curious about the person behind it.

---

## The style in one sentence

Write like you're telling a thoughtful friend the story of how you came to understand something — including the detours, the moments of confusion, and the surprising connections that only became visible in retrospect.

---

## Core principles

### 1. Lead with a scene, not a thesis

Sam opens Chapter 1 not with "this thesis is about X" but with a historical moment: John O'Keefe in the 1970s, 76 units, 68 of them ignored. Only then does he say: "The current thesis aims to tell the story of what's left out." The scene earns the claim.

**Do:** Open with a concrete moment — a discovery, an image, a puzzle — before the argument.  
**Avoid:** "In this chapter, I will demonstrate X."

---

### 2. First person, with intellectual stakes on the table

Sam uses "I" naturally, but what makes it work is that he puts his own intellectual struggles into the prose. Not just "I found" but "this objection troubled me for a long time," "a fear I had," "my personal discovery process." The reader cares because Sam clearly cares.

> *"Being a practitioner of this subfield, I also have my own skepticism."*

> *"I was fortunate to witness a beautiful example as the research unfolded in the Buzsáki lab."*

**Do:** Let the reader see what was surprising, what was hard to escape, what changed your mind.  
**Avoid:** Sanitizing the path. Discovery has texture.

---

### 3. Invite the reader to think alongside you

Sam frequently uses "we" when he means "you and I working through this together." He poses rhetorical questions not as decoration but as genuine invitations:

> *"What could be an alternative?"*  
> *"Why do we so often fail to recognize the relevant variable until the latent structure calls for an explanation?"*

He then answers the question. The question-then-answer rhythm makes complex ideas feel like conversation.

**Do:** Ask the question the reader is silently asking. Then answer it.  
**Avoid:** Proceeding as if the difficulty is obvious or already settled.

---

### 4. Reveal surprising things as surprises

Sam has a gift for the "big reveal." He builds a scenario — how *would* you design a steam-engine governor? — then pulls the floor away: *"The big reveal to the modern computation-minded reader is that this intuitive solution was not Watt's solution at all."* He earns the surprise by setting it up.

> *"There is a funny contradiction about such insights. During the encoding step we assume a sufficient match... During the decoding step, when the decoder makes a 'mistake', we interpret it as real scientific discovery."*

**Do:** Name the surprise. Say "strikingly," "interestingly," "the big reveal." Don't be coy.  
**Avoid:** Burying the interesting thing in technical prose.

---

### 5. Anchor abstractions in concrete examples — and make them count

Every abstract claim gets a concrete anchor. The bias-variance tradeoff → trial averaging. The Einstellung effect → chess masters failing to find the optimal move. The inside-out framework → Watt's centrifugal governor. These aren't illustrations dropped in as afterthoughts; they're load-bearing. The concrete example *is* the argument.

Sam also quotes directly — Wittgenstein on "slippery ice," Keynes on old ideas, Gibson on affordances — and these quotes feel like things he has lived with, not searched for to decorate a paragraph.

**Do:** Find one vivid example per big idea. Let it carry weight.  
**Avoid:** Saying "for example" and then giving something forgettable.

---

### 6. Explain why the writing is structured the way it is

Sam is unusually explicit about his own writerly choices:

> *"This introduction is formatted after one of the early inspirations for my interest in the mind: Gödel, Escher, Bach. In that Pulitzer-winning masterpiece, each theoretical chapter... is followed by a metaphorical dialogue... I decided to follow this style in order to show the tight interwoven nature of the two threads of my PhD work."*

This kind of meta-commentary — "here is why I am doing what I am doing" — creates intimacy. The reader feels like they are being let in.

**Do:** Occasionally explain a structural choice in your own voice.  
**Avoid:** Pretending the format is transparent and neutral.

---

### 7. Show your intellectual range as genuine, not decorative

Sam draws from Wittgenstein, Heidegger, Gibson, Keynes, Gödel Escher Bach, Buddhism, chess psychology — but none of it feels like name-dropping. It reads like these are things he has actually thought about. The Buddhism ending of Chapter 7 ("Buddha's raft") could easily feel pretentious; it doesn't, because he frames it as "another subject dear to my heart that is often considered 'woo-woo.'" He meets the reader's skepticism before it can form.

> *"As a 'renegade' from philosophy myself, I share many of these criticisms."*

**Do:** Bring in outside ideas you've genuinely lived with. Pre-empt the eye-roll.  
**Avoid:** Dropping references without showing how you've digested them.

---

### 8. Let discoveries arrive as discoveries

Sam describes his research in the past tense of someone looking back on the process of finding something, not delivering a pre-packaged result:

> *"Based on this initial finding, I then performed population decoding analysis to understand how goal information could be represented in the neural population. I found that action plans could be read out in two ways..."*

> *"I discovered latent variables... that correspond to when the animal performs non-locomotor off-maze behaviors... This would have been difficult for me to discover, as my prior mental set on hippocampal function was that it encodes spatial location."*

Notice: he tells you what he found, and then tells you why it would have been easy to miss it. That's the inside-out move applied to his own narrative.

**Do:** Include the path, not just the destination. What blocked you? What cleared it?  
**Avoid:** "Chapter X demonstrates Y." Present discoveries as things that happened to a person.

---

### 9. Mix sentence lengths deliberately

Sam alternates long, flowing sentences with very short punchy ones. The contrast creates rhythm and emphasis:

> *"This identity is simple but deep."* (after three lines of equations)

> *"The detail of the mechanics does not matter. What matters here is that its function does not require an accurate 'representation' of the state of the world."*

> *"Changes in the physical world translate into movement in the components of the governor. Not even the homeostatic 'goal' is represented as a number, but emerges from the interaction of the physical properties of the components."*

**Do:** Use short sentences to land a punch after a longer buildup.  
**Avoid:** Uniform sentence length throughout — it puts the reader to sleep.

---

### 10. End in the open, not the closed

Sam's conclusion doesn't wrap up neatly. It wanders into conjecture, admits what's still mysterious ("the benefit of the fragmented backbone seems much more mysterious. Here are some guesses."), and ends with a Buddhist parable about letting go of your own framework once it's no longer useful. The conclusion makes you feel like the work isn't over — like there's more to think about.

> *"If one day the inside-out ideas become a defunct raft, I shall peacefully let it go..."*

**Do:** End with an open question, a philosophical musing, or something that gestures toward what comes next.  
**Avoid:** "In conclusion, this chapter demonstrated X, Y, and Z." That's the raft you should have let go.

## Patterns to steal directly

- Open with a specific historical or scientific moment before stating the thesis.
- Name what was surprising or hard to escape in your own thinking.
- Ask "Why?" or "What could be an alternative?" and then answer.
- Use "But here is where magic happens" or "The big reveal is..." when something is genuinely surprising.
- Frame chapter previews as: "I found X, which mattered because..." rather than "Chapter X demonstrates Y."
- Quote something (a scientist, philosopher, your own reaction) when the quote does real work.
- Admit your prior assumptions before explaining how data changed them.
- End on something that opens outward, not closes down.

---

## What to avoid

- Chapter listings: "Chapter 2 does X, Chapter 3 does Y." (This is the Ji-An Li anti-pattern.)
- Passive voice as the default register.
- Technical precision as a substitute for engagement.
- Pretending the writing has no author.
- Overly even tone throughout — let yourself be surprised, puzzled, delighted.

---

## Tone in one word

**Warmly curious.** Not modest to the point of hedging, not confident to the point of coldness. The reader feels like they are thinking alongside someone who is genuinely excited about what they found — and honest about how they found it.
